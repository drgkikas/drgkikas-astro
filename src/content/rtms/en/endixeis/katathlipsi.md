Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (rTMS) is considered a major option in major depressive disorder (MDD) when the brain has not responded adequately to previous medications, or when antidepressant side effects are not tolerable. It is not necessarily intended for a first mild depressive episode, but it is an approved medical intervention for more complex cases.

During assessment, the psychiatrist maps your history to determine whether your depressive profile (with or without comorbid anxiety) is suitable for neuromodulation.

---

## 1. When it is indicated

rTMS is an officially approved treatment (FDA-approved since 2008) specifically for major depressive disorder. The clinical indication is stronger when a patient has received one or two classes of antidepressants (e.g., SSRIs or SNRIs) for adequate duration without full symptom remission.

Many patients present with depression and marked anxiety features (**anxious depression**), which makes pharmacotherapy more difficult. In the clinic, rTMS is adjusted through specific protocols (such as bilateral rTMS) to address **both** depressive mood and anxiety. If you have tried several medications without success, your condition is clinically described as "treatment-resistant" — see our guide specifically for [rTMS in Treatment-Resistant Depression (TRD)](/en/rtms/endixeis/trd).

## 2. Possible candidates

The ideal candidate for rTMS in depression:
- Feels that medication no longer lifts the "fog" or sadness.
- Suffers from significant medication side effects (weight gain, sedation, or sexual dysfunction).
- Has marked anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure from previously enjoyable activities).
- Has no non-removable metallic objects in the head (such as aneurysm clips) and no active epilepsy history.

## 3. How it fits within an overall treatment plan

You do not necessarily need to stop medication or psychotherapy to undergo rTMS. In most cases, magnetic treatment works **adjunctively**. The magnet "[wakes up](/en/rtms/core/ti-einai)" specific networks in the prefrontal cortex, helping the brain become responsive again. As sessions progress and symptoms improve, the physician may safely begin gradual medication reduction if appropriate, always under close monitoring.

## 4. What to expect in practice

Magnetic treatment for depression is an outpatient procedure. Your pathway is as follows:
- After detailed assessment, we decide whether to apply classic rTMS or faster iTBS.
- Usually, 15 to 30 sessions are scheduled (often daily). Learn all about [Cost and Procedure](/en/rtms/core/kostos-kai-diadikasia).
- Sessions last from 3 to 37 minutes and take place in a comfortable treatment chair.
- Immediately after each session, you return to your life (driving, work) without any dizziness or anesthesia.
