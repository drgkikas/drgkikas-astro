Cocaine Use Disorder (CUD) is a serious neuropsychiatric condition characterized by compulsive drug seeking, intense craving, loss of control, and repeated relapse despite negative consequences. To date, no pharmacological treatment has received regulatory approval for CUD — a gap that rTMS is convincingly starting to fill. Learn in detail [how rTMS works](/en/rtms/core/ti-einai).

---

## Why it matters

Cocaine addiction is associated with deep dysfunction of the prefrontal cortex (PFC) — the structure that exercises executive control over behavior. DLPFC hypofunction leads to an inability to inhibit the drive to use. The absence of effective pharmacotherapy makes neuromodulation techniques particularly attractive: rTMS can restore prefrontal executive control and directly reduce craving. Learn about [cost and procedure for addictions](/en/rtms/core/kostos-kai-diadikasia).

---

## Mechanism

- **Neurobiology of cocaine addiction**: Cocaine blocks dopamine reuptake in the nucleus accumbens (NAcc) → hyperdopaminergic activation of the reward circuit → gradual PFC hypofunction → failure of executive control.
- **rTMS target**: High-frequency (HF, 10–20 Hz) stimulation of the DLPFC → restoration of prefrontal activity → enhancement of top-down inhibition of the craving circuit. As in every protocol, [motor threshold measurement](/en/rtms/pos-leitourgei/motor-threshold) is essential.
- **Craving provocation**: Inducing craving before the session (e.g., through exposure to cues) enhances rTMS efficacy — the activated addictive circuit is more vulnerable to neuromodulation.
- **Reward circuit plasticity**: rTMS induces LTP-like [restoration of neuroplasticity](/en/rtms/pos-leitourgei/neuroplasticity) in the frontostriatal circuit, reducing long-term sensitivity to craving.

---

## Clinical Significance

The pivotal RCT (Terraneo et al., 2016) showed significant reduction in cocaine consumption and craving in patients treated with HF-rTMS DLPFC compared with conventional treatment. Subsequent studies (Bolloni et al., Antonelli et al.) confirmed craving reduction, with more modest results regarding long-term abstinence. rTMS is integrated as an adjunctive intervention in comprehensive addiction recovery programs — it does not replace psychosocial interventions but strengthens the executive control that supports them.
