The Motor Evoked Potential (MEP) is the electromyographic response recorded in a peripheral muscle after TMS of the corresponding motor representation in the primary motor cortex. It is the "gold standard" for measuring cortical excitability and stimulation efficacy in clinical rTMS practice. It is a fundamental parameter for understanding [rTMS physiology](/en/rtms/pos-leitourgei/neuromodulation).

### Why it matters
The MEP measures something that is not visible: the actual brain response to stimulation. Without the MEP, the clinician cannot know whether the applied intensity activates the cortex sufficiently. Furthermore, changes in MEP amplitude or latency during a treatment cycle provide a neurophysiological marker of neuroplasticity.

### Mechanism
- A TMS pulse induces trans-synaptic stimulation of Betz cells (Layer V pyramidal neurons) in M1.
- The electrical signal descends through the corticospinal tract → corresponding peripheral neuron → muscle contraction.
- EMG electrodes record the MEP from the APB/FDI with a typical latency of ~20–25ms (for the hand). See the [detailed measurement procedure](/en/rtms/core/kostos-kai-diadikasia) in the clinic.
- **Amplitude:** Reflects the number and synchrony of activated motor units — increase = increased excitability.
- **Latency:** Central conduction time — pathological increase (in demyelinating diseases).
- MEP-based cortical silent period (CSP) measures GABAergic inhibition.

### Clinical Significance
Primary use: Determining [motor threshold](/en/rtms/pos-leitourgei/motor-threshold) (RMT/AMT) to calibrate rTMS intensity. Secondary use: Monitoring neuroplasticity and evaluating protocol efficacy. In a research context: Biomarker for TRD outcome prediction.
