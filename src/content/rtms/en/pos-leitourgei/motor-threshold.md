Motor Threshold (MT) is defined as the minimum magnetic stimulation intensity required to elicit a visible or measurable motor response in the corresponding muscle in ≥50% of trials. It is the central indicator for individualizing every rTMS protocol — without correct MT calibration, treatment cannot be applied safely or effectively. Learn in detail how [intensity individualization in rTMS](/en/rtms/core/kostos-kai-diadikasia) is achieved.

### Why it matters
Every brain has different excitability — due to anatomy, age, medication, and pathological state. MT corrects for this difference: if two patients receive exactly the same intensity (e.g., 60% machine power) but have different MTs, the actual cortical stimulation is radically different. The expression "X% MT" ensures that every patient receives a physiologically equivalent dose. This process is critical for [rTMS safety](/en/rtms/core/asfaleia-parenergeies).

### Mechanism
- **Target muscle:** Usually Abductor Pollicis Brevis (APB) or First Dorsal Interosseous (FDI) of the dominant hand.
- **Stimulation:** TMS coil over the primary motor cortex (M1) — hand area.
- **RMT (Resting Motor Threshold):** Measurement at rest — minimum intensity for [MEP measurement](/en/rtms/pos-leitourgei/mep) (Motor Evoked Potential) ≥50μV in 5/10 trials.
- **AMT (Active Motor Threshold):** Measurement while the muscle maintains a light contraction (~10% Maximum Voluntary Contraction) — generally ~10–15% lower than RMT.
- **Influenced by:** Medication (BZD increase MT, antiepileptics increase MT), age, fatigue, sleep cycle.
- Must be remeasured if significant medication changes occur.

### Clinical Significance
rTMS protocols always define intensity as a percentage of MT (e.g., 110% RMT for HF-rTMS, 80% AMT for iTBS). This way of expression allows comparison between research centers and physiologically accurate application at the clinical level.
